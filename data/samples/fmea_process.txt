Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEA) - Process Guide

FMEA is a systematic method for identifying potential failure modes in a process, assessing their risk, and prioritizing corrective actions.

Types of FMEA:
- Design FMEA (DFMEA): Analyzes potential failures in product design
- Process FMEA (PFMEA): Analyzes potential failures in manufacturing processes

Risk Priority Number (RPN):
RPN = Severity x Occurrence x Detection

Severity (S) Scale (1-10):
1: No effect
2-3: Minor effect, slight inconvenience
4-6: Moderate effect, customer experiences some dissatisfaction
7-8: High effect, customer very dissatisfied
9-10: Hazardous, potential safety issue without/with warning

Occurrence (O) Scale (1-10):
1: Failure unlikely (< 1 in 1,500,000)
2-3: Low failure rate (1 in 150,000 to 1 in 15,000)
4-6: Moderate failure rate (1 in 2,000 to 1 in 80)
7-8: High failure rate (1 in 20 to 1 in 8)
9-10: Very high failure rate (1 in 3 to 1 in 2)

Detection (D) Scale (1-10):
1-2: Almost certain detection, automated inspection with error-proofing
3-4: High detection, automated inspection
5-6: Moderate detection, manual inspection or SPC monitoring
7-8: Low detection, visual inspection only
9: Very low detection, indirect or random checks
10: No detection method available

PFMEA Process Steps:
1. Identify the process steps and their functions
2. Identify potential failure modes for each step
3. Determine potential effects of each failure
4. Assign Severity rating
5. Identify potential causes of each failure mode
6. Assign Occurrence rating
7. Identify current process controls (prevention and detection)
8. Assign Detection rating
9. Calculate RPN
10. Prioritize actions based on RPN and severity
11. Implement corrective actions
12. Re-assess RPN after actions

Action Priority:
- RPN > 200: Immediate corrective action required
- RPN 120-200: Action required, plan improvement
- RPN 80-120: Monitor and consider improvement
- RPN < 80: Acceptable, continue monitoring

Best Practices:
- Start PFMEA during process planning, not after production starts
- Include cross-functional team members (engineering, quality, production, maintenance)
- Review and update PFMEA whenever process changes occur
- Focus on high-severity items regardless of RPN
- Link FMEA to control plans and work instructions
- Use FMEA as a living document, not a one-time exercise

Connection to Other Quality Tools:
- Control Plan: Actions from FMEA drive control plan requirements
- SPC: High-risk characteristics identified by FMEA should be monitored with SPC
- 8D Problem Solving: FMEA helps prevent recurrence identified in 8D
- PPAP: FMEA is a required element of the Production Part Approval Process
